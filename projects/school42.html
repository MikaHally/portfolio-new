Eat. Code. Sleep. Repeat.

For the uninitiated, 42 Silicon Valley is an elite engineering college in the San Francisco Bay Area. There is no other institution of higher education like School 42. Anyone between the age of 18 and 30 can apply for admission. No prior coding experience is necessary. Moreover, there are no teachers, only administration staff. Everyone learns by doing, focusing on assignments, working together in a peer-to-peer environment with the latest state of the art technology. It’s highly competitive and its students are some of the most sought after in the industry.
Applicants that successfully completed the online math and logic test, travel the world at their own expense to participate in the Piscine, a month-long tryout, with the sole aim to get accepted into their full-time study program. The Piscine which means swimming pool in french is hard, demanding and very intense. It is comparable with being pushed into a swimming pool without being able to swim. Dare to struggle, dare to win.

Goal

So why did I even bother to spend one entire month starring at a computer screen for 15 hours a day and only getting about 6 hours of sleep in between? During my first two semesters of studying Product Management I learned what makes a good product manager. One quality of an excellent PM is the ability to communicate with engineers efficiently. I however only had very little technical background up to that point. I was dedicated to change that. So I set out to Silicon Valley to challenge myself and learn as much as possible about that topic.

What makes an excellent PM

Product managers sit at the center of UX, technology and business. Therefore they have to do a lot of diverse things well.

Qualities

  • Outstanding intellectual ability, able to process and synthesize information
  • Excellent communication
  • Demonstrated leadership
  • Collaborative and effective within the company culture
  • Knack for knowing what users / customers want
  • Strategic / analytical thinking
  • Technical background
  • Entrepreneurial spirit

The usual day at 42 begins every morning at 8:42 AM. The school day ends at 11:42 PM with everybody applauding to express appreciation to people around and gain a strength for new challenges of a new day. One is expected to work an average of 12 to 14 hours a day, every day of the week. I routinely devoted 15 hours a day to the challenges at 42. In spite of the ridiculously long hours, one often does not complete all of their daily assignments. Every morning’s assignments must be completed by 11:42 PM the next day. This means days overlap and one ends up juggling a multitude of assignments they can’t get their hands around. At the outset, attendees learn the basics of a powerful command-line interface, into which you can type text commands to perform specific tasks. Then one tackle the fundamentals of C programming, to write different sorts of functions and libraries. And finish the course with sophisticated exercises like recursion, structures, binary trees and etc.. Friday evenings are exam nights which is a hell of a way to spend a Friday night.

After four very demanding weeks I received an acceptance email from School 42. I struggled, I was frustrated, I learned and grew as a person. It was one of the best spent months of my life. All my efforts paid off and I made very good friends along the way. However, I nonetheless decided to continue my Bachelors in Product Management at CODE University in Berlin.

Now after being back in Germany for almost 2 month I am looking back at those 4-weeks and I have to admit that much to my astonishment I learned so much more beyond coding.
At some point I read an article in which the author reveals his love to surfing (the sport) and coding and draws parallels between both of them. As I am a surfing enthusiast myself I could not agree more with him. Let me explain: Before you can reach the enjoyable moment of riding a wave you have to get to the lineup. This means you have to swallow a lot of salty water and paddle to exhaustion only to get to the take-off zone. What does that now has to do with coding you may ask? Everything! Waves are like coding challenges, you can face them or freak out and turn around if they are too big or if you have bad day. You can try to ride them, fail, go back on your computer and try again. You repeat this process as long as you finally get to the level where it all becomes an easy game. Coding requires inordinate patience, research, focus, and persistence. But the lessons learned allow you to progress. Coding also taught me to ultimately break down every problem to the last bit that one is facing. That helps to make the problem itself look simpler and more approachable. This is not only helpful for programming but also for product management and everyday life scenarios.
